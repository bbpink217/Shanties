23:25:13

· As a sea elf, it's no surprise that I'd spent most of my life near the water. The sea was there when I was born. The cry of seabirds accompanied by the ocean's endless song was like a lullaby to me, and the gentle rock of waves under hull could always bring me to a soft state of repose. I had grown up on that ship. Sea-faring wasn't just a way of life for me, it became my nature. Learning to speak and walk was nearly as important as it was to sail and hunt. And all the while, the sea offered its bounties and led us to sights that would make even the sternest of sailors go wide-eyed with wonder. The sea was forgiving and free, sometimes in places in which the ship was not. Everyone had a role to play in the rhythmic, day-to-day flow of crewmanship. Being young and unskilled, I was in charge of cleaning the mess hall at first, but soon, I was deemed "skilled enough" to be a hunter, and then a swordsman. And to this day, I still remember the look on my father's face when I out-sparred the best of our crew. I beamed when he could only speak in utterly baffled tones: "Well, I'll be damned...your mother would be proud."

23:25:30

· Years had passed since then, but one day, my father could no longer keep up with the sea-faring life. It had begun to drift from him like an ebbing current, and it was time for his position to be replaced. And in the moment it was passed onto me, after the fleeting courage and pride, came havoc and lies. The reins of the ship had been let loose; she snapped her jaws and fought against me; a snarling, writhing beast that would shake away from my weak grip. It felt like all the gales on this wild, untamable main could come and laugh at me, to tangle up our sails and blind me, until the only thing left on my lips were "well, I'll be damned." To be a first mate requires not just the ability to hold a blade with purpose. It requires a confluence of wisdom, boldness, and sheer will. But I, being young and foolish, was oblivious to the responsibilities of upholding such an important position. But then one night, I recalled the hopeless look that rended my captain's face as he witnessed my struggle. I wondered, alone in my quiet quarters and surrounded in tenuous candlelight, if I was doomed for having my position revoked. Nearly a week had passed since I'd been handed this role, and quicker than an anchor, I was sinking. I needed to get my head above the water, I needed to make her proud. And that's what made all the difference.
